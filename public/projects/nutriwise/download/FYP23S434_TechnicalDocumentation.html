  1. Project Background

  1. Introduction

The ever-evolving field of mobile technology is seeing an increase in awareness of the healthcare role that applications may play, especially in the management of chronic illnesses like diabetes.

This realisation prompted the development of "NUTRIWISE," a smartphone application made expressly to help people—particularly the elderly—manage their blood sugar levels and eating patterns. The advent of such technology is particularly timely, since diabetes is becoming more and more common in the elderly population, which presents many health issues. These difficulties are made worse by the obstacles that older persons must overcome, like physical restrictions, cognitive decline, and lack of technical literacy.

With its age-friendly interface, "NUTRIWISE" is envisioned as a solution that not only overcomes these obstacles but also fits in with Singapore's unique eating habits and way of life. This programme attempts to offer a comprehensive solution for improving health outcomes and quality of life for older persons with diabetes by combining dietary tracking with health management.

We will go into great depth about our project in this document, providing a thorough summary of our study and the choices we have taken along the way. Our goal is to bring you a glimpse into our minds and the reasoning behind each feature's inclusion.

  1. Problem Statement

Even with the abundance of health applications available, there is still a sizable market gap, especially in the context of Singapore. It is difficult for people, especially the elderly, to accurately log meals and manage their condition because current technologies frequently do not correspond with local dietary patterns and lifestyle. The intricacy of the current programmes, which can be daunting for older users or those with less technological proficiency, makes this disparity more noticeable.

This group makes up a sizable portion of the population with diabetes. Furthermore, the global issue for diabetic individuals lies in the intricacy of self-management, which frequently results in worse than ideal health outcomes and a lower standard of living. The lack of a complete, intuitive mobile application that integrates blood glucose monitoring and nutrition tracking creates a need for a trustworthy instrument that can support well-informed decision-making.

By creating "NUTRIWISE," an intuitive, tailored application centred on the Singaporean environment, our project seeks to close this gap and enable users to better manage their diabetes and take charge of their health.

  1. Understanding Diabetes

Diabetes is a chronic condition characterised by high blood sugar levels brought on by the body's inability to make or use insulin, a hormone that controls blood sugar levels. Type 1 diabetes is caused by the body's immune system attacking cells that produce insulin, while Type 2 diabetes is connected to lifestyle factors that result in insulin resistance. Prediabetes and gestational diabetes are two other forms of the disease. Diabetes can lead to serious complications like cardiovascular disease, neuropathy, and kidney damage if it is not treated.

  1. Diabetes In Singapore

By 2030, Singapore is expected to see a significant increase in its diabetic population, with projections indicating a rise from approximately 400,000 to 600,000 among residents over the age of 40. This is a 50% increase, meaning that 200,000 more people will be managing the condition than they already are. Since there is currently no cure for diabetes, which accounted for 1.7% of all deaths in Singapore in 2011, it is imperative that the illness be brought under control as soon as possible. These figures echo a global concern, as highlighted by the World Health Organisation’s estimate that over 346 million people worldwide suffer from diabetes, marking it as a major health challenge of our time.

  1. Type 1 Diabetes (T1D)

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the body’s immune system mistakenly attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.  Consequently, little to no insulin is produced by those who have type 1 diabetes. It is usually diagnosed in children and adolescents but can also appear in adults. For management, an insulin pump or daily insulin injections are necessary. It is thought that a mix of environmental variables, such as viral infections, and genetic susceptibility is the cause.

  1. Type 2 Diabetes (T2D)

Type 2 diabetes is the most common form of diabetes, accounting for about 90% of all diabetes cases. It is marked by the body's poor use of insulin, frequently in conjunction with decreased insulin synthesis. This form is strongly associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, genetic factors and age. Even though it mostly affects adults, more younger people are receiving diagnoses as a result of rising obesity prevalence. Modifications in food and lifestyle, oral medicines, and occasionally insulin therapy are all part of management.

  1. Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes occurs in some pregnant women without a previous history of diabetes. Blood sugar levels rise because their bodies are unable to manufacture enough insulin to satisfy the increasing demands during pregnancy. It raises the possibility of difficulties developing during pregnancy and childbirth. It usually goes away after delivery, but it raises the chance of getting T2D later in life. Blood sugar monitoring and dietary changes are the mainstays of management, though insulin or oral medications may be required in some cases.

  1. Prediabetes

Prediabetes is a condition where blood sugar levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be considered diabetes. People who have prediabetes have a higher chance of developing type 2 diabetes, heart disease, and stroke. It acts as a warning indicator, prompting dietary adjustments and increased exercise as lifestyle treatments to delay the onset of type 2 diabetes. Regular monitoring can help detect any progression early, allowing for timely interventions.

  1. Other Specific Types

There are less common types of diabetes besides the main ones that are brought on by certain genetic disorders, surgeries, drugs, infections, and other medical situations. Each rare form has distinct causes and treatments, and only accounts for a small percentage of diabetes cases.

The research paper reveals that the vast majority of patients registered had Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM), with only a small fraction (less than 1%) presenting with Type 1 or other forms of diabetes, which include drug-induced, gestational, monogenic, and secondary diabetes. The research paper indicates that in Singapore, the prevalence of diabetes is predominantly observed in individuals over the age of 44, a demographic that largely encompasses the elderly population. Consequently, 91.3% of diabetes cases are found in adults older than 44 years. This comparison underscores a common global pattern wherein diabetes is more prevalent with increasing age.

  1. Blood Glucose

When food high in carbohydrates is consumed, it is absorbed into the blood as glucose and carried into the liver, which half of it is transported into the entire body. The pancreas releases insulin as a reaction to the increase of sugar in the blood. The insulin transports the glucose to cells throughout the body that absorb and use it. Consuming too much glucose might cause the body to produce too much insulin. The body stores fat in its tissues as a result of this increased insulin, which encourages fat synthesis and inhibits fat breakdown. Several hours after a meal, blood sugar levels may not drop if there is a disruption in the blood’s ability to handle sugar, such as when insulin resistance or insufficient insulin synthesis occurs. Hence, it is important to not eat meals that don't trigger abrupt spikes in blood sugar levels.

  1. Foods to Avoid

A common misconception is that foods high in calories always cause blood sugar levels to rise, however this isnt always the case. Diets heavy in carbs have the biggest effect on blood sugar levels as they are quickly converted into energy. Bread, rice, fruits and sweet foods are a few examples. Following which are foods high in protein like meats, fish, eggs, dairy products and fatty foods. Though carbohydrates affect blood sugar levels, it is important to remember that cutting it out entirely can lead to an unbalanced diet and a lack of fullness after meals, which could potentially lead to overindulgence in foods high in fat and protein.

  1. Nutrients to Consume

The ideal ratio of fat, protein, and carbohydrates depends on the specific metabolic objectives and preferences of the diabetic, according to the evidence that is now available. The most crucial thing to remember while trying to lose or maintain weight is the total number of calories.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates have the most impact on glycemic response, however, the ideal amount is unclear as there are different opinions regarding recommendations for low Carbohydrates diets. The latest research by the ADA states that low carbohydrates eating plans results in improved glycemia and the potential to reduce antihyperglycemic medications for patients with type 2 diabetes. Though, diets lower in Carbohydrates are difficult to sustain in the long run with few long-term studies to show extended benefits.

Fibre

It is recommended for patients with diabetes to consume 20 to 35g of fibre from raw vegetables and unprocessed grains per day. A meal high in fibre is processed more slowly, increasing satiety. It may also contain less calories and added sugars, reducing the danger of obesity and raising the possibility of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and colon cancer. Diabetics should consume at least half of all grains as whole grains, which includes legumes, whole grain breads, cereals, whole fruits and vegetables.

Fat

There is conflicting data regarding the optimal total fat intake for diabetics; so, individualised goals are necessary, especially as fat quality seems to be far more significant than quantity. As there is a high risk of cardiovascular disease in diabetic patients, the goals for dietary fat intake is similar to people with cardiovascular disease without diabetes. The American Heart Association and American College of Cardiology recommends limiting the amount of dietary saturated and trans-fat intake. Recommendations from the Institute of Medicine and the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics says that 20% to 35% of total calories should come from fat.

Protein

Currently, there is no evidence regarding the optimal amount of dietary protein to optimise glycemic control or cardiovascular disease risk. The ADA Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes-2019 states that adjusting the daily level of protein intake will not necessarily improve health in individuals with diabetes. As a result, protein consumption objectives ought to be customised according to present eating habits. The Joslin Diabetes Center advises a protein intake of 20-30% of total energy intake for individuals with diabetes.

  1. Carbohydrate Counting

Any food’s carbohydrates have an impact on blood glucose levels. Carbohydrate monitoring through carb counting, the exchange technique or experience-based estimation is a crucial tactic for glycemic control improvement and medication timing. The idea behind the carbohydrate counting system is that every serving of carbohydrate is equivalent to about 15gm of carbohydrates. Additionally, it allows diabetics to match their carbohydrate intake to the proper mealtime insulin dose by setting a goal carbohydrate consumption for each meal. Improved glucose control, flexibility in food selection, a better knowledge of insulin dosage, and ease of meal planning are some potential benefits of CHO counting. However, diabetics have to be motivated in order to complete the carbohydrate counting.

Regular medical check-ups, addressing co-existing health conditions, and ensuring mental well-being are also integral components. Both preventive and management measures aim to avert complications and ensure a healthier life for individuals at risk of or living with diabetes.

  1. Insulin for Diabetes

Insulin is a vital hormone naturally produced by the pancreas, specifically by beta cells. Its main function is to help move glucose from the bloodstream into the body's cells, where it is used as an energy source, so that blood glucose levels are kept within a healthy range. Glucose builds up in the bloodstream rather than going into the cells to produce energy when there is not enough insulin.

When a person has Type 1 diabetes, their body produces no insulin at all, thus daily insulin injections are required to keep their bodies functioning properly. Conversely, type 2 diabetes usually results from either insufficient insulin synthesis or decreased insulin sensitivity. In certain situations, administering insulin injections may be necessary to control blood glucose levels.

  1. Types of Insulin

Insulin is classified according to how long it acts in the body. Rapid- or short-acting insulin is designed to help lower blood glucose levels during mealtimes, while intermediate or long-acting insulin serves to manage the body's total insulin needs. Both kinds are essential for regulating blood sugar levels.

To properly manage their diabetes, many people need to take both rapid-acting and longer-acting insulin. It's critical to keep in mind that each person has individual insulin requirements, which may require varying combinations of various insulin types.

  1. Calculating Insulin Dosage

Normally, a bolus insulin(rapid acting) dose is needed to be estimated depending on the amount of carbohydrates consumed. A general rule of thumb states that 1 unit of rapid-acting insulin is needed to process 12 to 15 grams of carbohydrates. A bolus dose is also used to lower high blood glucose levels, using a general rule that 1 unit of insulin lowers blood glucose levels by 50 milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL). However, it is worth noting that everyone processes insulin differently and it is important to adjust based on how one’s body responds to the insulin. To calculate insulin intake, one can use the following guideline:

1.

Firstly, do a check on your blood glucose levels before a meal. If the levels are within the target range, no extra insulin is needed. However, if the blood glucose level is higher than the target range (usually around 120), you can use an amount of insulin following the rule of thumb, 1 unit of fast acting insulin will lower blood glucose levels by 50.

2.

Next, you would need to calculate the amount of carbohydrates you’re about to consume. To estimate the amount of insulin needed, one can use the carbohydrate counter guide, for every 12-15 grams of carbohydrates, 1 unit of rapid-acting insulin is recommended. Please note that this amount is different for each individual.

3.

Calculate the amount needed to reach your target range according to your meal

4.

Lastly, you can administer the amount of insulin calculated.

If a diabetic consumes roughly the same amount of carbohydrates each day, they can inject the same amounts of insulin daily, barring any special influence, like drinking alcohol or doing exercises. It takes some time to figure out how their body responds to insulin.

  1. Hypoglycemia Prevention

Hypoglycemia occurs in diabetics using insulin and oral antihyperglycemic medications. The following is a guideline for diabetics to help prevent hypoglycemia:

1.

When taking insulin or oral diabetes medication, consistency with the amount consumed and timing of meals is important. Do not skip or delay meals.

2.

Check your blood glucose levels several times a week or multiple times a day depending on the treatment plan. The only way to guarantee that blood sugar levels stay within a person’s goal range is to monitor them carefully.

3.

Take prescription on schedule and adhere to the doctor's advice on medication as directed.

4.

If physical activity increases, take additional food or modify medication according to the results of the blood sugar tests and nature and duration of the exercise.

5.

When having an alcoholic beverage, it is recommended to consume a meal or snack. Hypoglycemia can occur as a result of alcohol consumption on an empty stomach.

6.

Take down reactions to low glucose as it can assist the medical team in determining the tendencies that lead to hypoglycemia and how to avoid them.

7.

Always carry some form of diabetes identification so that people will know you have diabetes in an emergency.

  1. Hypoglycemia Treatment

Hypoglycemia is defined as having low blood glucose less than 70mg/dL. Anxiety, anger, dizziness, and shakiness are some of the symptoms. Headache, double vision, clumsiness, fury, and oral numbness are examples of advanced symptoms. Glucose must be administered right away to treat hypoglycemia. Adhere to the 15/15 rule: 15 grams of simple carbs should raise blood sugar levels by 30 to 45 milligrammes per decilitre in 15 minutes. Diabetics should be instructed to immediately consume one of the following "quick fix" items to boost blood glucose when it falls below 70 mg/dL:

  • Glucose tablets
  • Gel tube
  • 4 ounces of juice or regular soda
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar, honey or corn syrup
  • Hard candy, jelly beans or gumdrops

Blood glucose should be checked again after 15 minutes to ensure that it is increasing until 70mg/dL. Thereafter, a snack should be consumed if the next meal is over an hour away.

  1. Conclusion

Based on our research findings, which indicate that 98.9% of diabetics have type 2 diabetes, our primary focus will revolve around preventive measures and support mechanisms within our application. Furthermore, as 93.8% of our user base is 44 years of age and older, with a sizable percentage of senior citizens, we are committed to designing an interface that meets their particular requirements. Our design strategy will give top priority to outstanding visibility and ease of use in order to accommodate people who might not have extensive technical capabilities.

It is critical to comprehend how various macronutrients affect the management of diabetes. Diabetics need to know how to precisely measure calories and organise their meals in order to keep their blood sugar levels within the suggested range. The capacity to give the right quantity of insulin in situations where glucose levels are above the limit is essential and may even save lives. Thus, our app will make it easier to track macronutrients, calculate insulin dosages, and count calories. By offering these crucial functions, we hope to empower our users for both everyday life and emergency situations. Accuracy in these computations is essential to guarantee our users' security and welfare.

  1. Conclusion

We have gained important insights into a variety of applications and design interfaces thanks to our thorough study. We came across a number of approaches to food logging during our investigation into health applications, which brought to light the difficulties posed by inconsistent and inaccurate food scanning features.

We saw that interfaces designed with older users in mind were common in the field of apps for the elderly. Vibrant colours, easily readable typefaces, and user-friendly iconography were used in these interfaces to improve navigation for older users. We also realised how important it is to have a simple setup procedure because complicated configurations might discourage consumers and lead to dissatisfaction.

We learned more about the complex interaction between insulin intake and blood glucose levels in the diabetes application area, and we also determined what kinds of data are necessary for people with diabetes. We looked at the data collection and presentation methods used by these apps, pointing out that they have notification features that help users remember their daily responsibilities and critical metrics like blood sugar, caloric intake, and insulin dosage.

Nevertheless, we also saw significant issues with some of the applications, such as cluttered home screens and the need for users to enter unknown calorie information. Additionally, some programmes made entering all three of the necessary bits of information required, which could complicate things too much. In addition, we examined the kinds of reports that these apps produced, realising how crucial it is to offer data that medical experts and carers can review to comprehend a patient's day-to-day activities.

Lastly, we also realised that if we were to hide functions behind paywalls or membership services, our user base could find it difficult to access essential features. Our approach to creating a diabetes management application that tackles these issues and gives priority to user-friendly interfaces and necessary functionalities will be informed by these insights.

  1. Calculating of Nutritional Information

Unfortunately, it is currently difficult for diabetics, especially the elderly, to receive appropriate nutritional information for meals without actively searching for specific ingredients and conducting calculations. Our analysis shows that current applications provide only approximations for pre-measured meals in their databases. In addition to being the most user-friendly alternative at the moment, our system will have a portion size indication that will let users modify serving sizes in order to get the most accurate statistics possible. Therefore, we are using the nutritional information from the Singapore Health Promotion Board as our gauge, which provides nutritional information based on portion sizes and contains food that are local to Singaporeans.

Scrum is an Agile framework that helps teams collaborate and get high-impact work done. It is a flexible and iterative system designed for change and uncertainty, it features a ‘Rolling wave’ process which is a continuous plan-execute-evaluate cycle.   1. Flexibility & Adaptability: Scrum allows us to respond promptly to changes in project requirements or user feedback. This is crucial for our mobile app development in which our requirements and features may change over time.

  1. Iterative Development: The iterative nature of Scrum, which emphasises on short development cycles or sprints, facilitates regulated inspections and adaptations. This iterative process ensures that the project remains on course and that adjustments can be made promptly.

  1. Communication & Collaboration: The daily Scrum allows our team to have the habit to communicate, identify roadblocks, solve problems and create ideas.
